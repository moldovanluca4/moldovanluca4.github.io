Building Software Around Mainframe Systems

My summer internship at Living Mainframe GmbH gave me the opportunity to move from learning about enterprise computing in theory to experiencing it in a professional environment. What made this opportunity especially meaningful is that I had just completed my first year of studies, a stage where very few companies are willing to trust a student with no real exposure to industry work. For that reason, I deeply appreciate the Living Mainframe team for giving me the chance to learn, grow, and be part of a professional environment so early in my academic journey.

Before this experience, I had already developed an interest in IBM Z and mainframe technology through the student ambassador program. However, the internship helped me understand these systems from a much more practical perspective. I gained a better understanding of mainframe systems, IBM z/OS, IBM Db2, and IBM products in general, while also seeing how enterprise software is built, maintained, and connected to real business workflows.

One of the most valuable parts of the internship was seeing how lower-level programming can connect with higher-level application design. I worked with C programming, Python, Flask, and SQLite in a way that made the system feel like a complete software product rather than a collection of separate tools. This taught me to think more carefully about how data moves through a program, how different layers communicate, and how a technical idea becomes something a user can interact with.

During the internship, I also had the chance to learn how to write COBOL code and compile it using JCL. This experience made me much more grateful for the modern development tools we have today, while also giving me a stronger respect for the developers who built reliable systems with far fewer conveniences. It showed me how much discipline, precision, and technical skill enterprise computing has required for decades.

Being included in official team meetings was another important part of my growth. It helped me develop professionally by observing how technical discussions happen in a company setting, how decisions are communicated, and how teams organize their work. Speaking openly with colleagues, asking questions, and shadowing the projects they were working on helped me understand not only the technical side of software engineering, but also the collaboration and communication behind it.

I also had a very insightful conversation with the team's project manager, which opened my eyes to the importance and difficulty of project management. It made me realize that managing a project is not only about assigning tasks, but also about coordinating people, priorities, deadlines, communication, and expectations. That conversation helped me understand that project management is often the hidden backbone of every successful project.

The experience strengthened my respect for enterprise systems. Mainframes are often described as old technology from the outside, but working closer to the ecosystem showed me how much robustness, reliability, and careful engineering goes into environments that support important business processes. It made me more interested in the connection between systems programming, backend development, databases, and the operational reality of software used by organizations.

Looking back, the Living Mainframe internship was an important step in shaping my technical direction. It connected my interest in mainframe computing with practical software engineering and gave me more confidence in building systems that combine low-level logic, backend processing, databases, and web interfaces into one coherent application. More importantly, it showed me how valuable it is when a team gives a young student the chance to learn seriously, contribute, and grow through real professional exposure.
